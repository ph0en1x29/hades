# Abstract

Large Language Models are increasingly deployed for automated alert triage in Security Operations Centers, processing thousands of SIEM alerts that human analysts cannot review at scale. We systematize and evaluate a critical vulnerability first demonstrated by Neaves [2025]: adversaries can embed prompt injection payloads in network traffic fields — HTTP headers, DNS queries, TLS certificates — that SIEM systems log and feed to triage models.

We present **Hades**, an evaluation framework for measuring and defending against adversarial manipulation of LLM-based SOC systems. Using 12,147 rule-linked alerts across 27 MITRE ATT&CK techniques, we generate 1.4 million adversarial variants across 12 injection vectors and 5 attack classes. Our framework targets 4 open-weight MoE models (DeepSeek R1, GLM-5, Kimi K2.5, Qwen 3.5) under three attacker knowledge levels.

Hades introduces three novel components: (1) a **correlator agent** detecting multi-stage campaigns through IP clustering and technique chain matching; (2) **behavioral invariant defenses** that detect phantom IPs, fabricated references, and suspicious confidence patterns in triage outputs, auto-escalating suspected injections without relying on model-level defenses that adaptive attackers consistently bypass (Nasr et al., 2025); and (3) a **SOC-Bench adapter** for standardized ring-scored evaluation.

Normalization analysis shows direct misclassification and confidence manipulation payloads survive 100% of SIEM normalization, while evasion encodings defeat keyword defenses yet remain LLM-interpretable. Preliminary evaluation on simulated triage outputs shows behavioral invariants detecting 100% of direct misclassification (C1) and reasoning corruption (C3), 98% on attention hijacking (C4), with 0% false positives. Pipeline dry-run validation shows adversarial injection can degrade Fox campaign scores by 44.7 points under simulated conditions (95.7→51.0/100).

Corroborating evidence: Neaves (2025) demonstrated successful injection through HTTP User-Agent and SSH usernames in production SIEMs; Unit 42 (2026) reports 22 indirect prompt injection techniques in the wild. We release Hades as open-source tooling for evaluating LLM-based security automation.
